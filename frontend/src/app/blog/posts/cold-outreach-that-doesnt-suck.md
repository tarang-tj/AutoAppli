Every student running an internship search sends roughly the same cold message. It looks like this:

> Hi [Name], I hope this email finds you well. My name is [You] and I’m a junior at [School] majoring in Computer Science. I came across [Company] and was very impressed by the innovative work being done. I would love to learn more about opportunities and how I might contribute. I have attached my resume for your reference. Looking forward to hearing from you!

This message is a black hole. Recruiters and engineers get fifteen of these a week. They don’t reply. They might not even open it. Worse — over time, they associate “[School] junior” with this template, which means the next student from your school who reaches out also gets ignored.

Here’s what actually gets a reply.

## The four parts that matter

A cold message is short. It does four things and then stops:

1. A subject line that’s not “Quick question”
2. An opener that proves you’re a real person who picked them on purpose
3. A specific, small ask
4. A polite exit

That’s it. No “I hope this finds you well.” No bio paragraph. No attached resume. No “I would love the opportunity to leverage my passion for innovative solutions.”

## Subject lines

Bad subjects every recruiter has muted: “Quick question”, “Internship inquiry”, “Reaching out”, “Hello”.

What works: short, specific, and *about them or their work*. Some that have actually gotten replies:

- `Question about your team’s switch to Rust`
- `CMU junior — your 2024 talk on incident response`
- `2-min question about the data infra role`

The recruiter or engineer should be able to read the subject and know within a second why you specifically wrote them and what you want.

## The opener (this is the whole game)

There are three opener types, ranked by reply rate.

### Tier 1: Connection

You share a real connection. Same lab, same advisor, mutual friend, same class. *Real* connection — not “we both went to Stanford” if Stanford has 17,000 students.

> Hey [Name] — I work in Professor Liu’s lab too. She mentioned you graduated last year and now do platform work at Stripe.

This is a 60%+ reply rate opener. Use it whenever you have it. If you don’t have it, don’t fake it.

### Tier 2: Context

You read their work and have a specific reaction to a specific thing they did. Their public talk, their PR comment on a popular OSS repo, their blog post, their company’s recent launch.

> Watched your talk at Strange Loop on building durable workflows. The bit about idempotency keys at the database layer (vs application layer) made me rethink a side project I’m working on.

Note the specificity — not “I loved your talk”, but the *specific moment* in the talk that landed. This is hard to fake and recruiters can tell when it’s been faked. Worth the 15 minutes of actual research.

### Tier 3: Curiosity

You have a real question about their work that you can’t easily answer with Google. Not “what’s it like working at [Company]” — that’s a Glassdoor question and you’ve insulted them by asking. A real, technical, narrow question.

> Curious — when your team migrated to gRPC, did you keep REST endpoints up in parallel during the cutover or do a hard switch? Asking because I’m doing something similar at smaller scale and the dual-stack maintenance is killing me.

This works because it treats them like a peer with expertise, not a gatekeeper with a job to give you. Sometimes they reply with the answer and never get to the “and by the way I’m looking for an internship” part — that’s fine, you’ve now started a relationship.

## The ask

Ask for one specific small thing. Not “an opportunity at your company”. Specific small things that work:

- A 15-minute call to ask 3 questions
- An honest read on whether your background fits the [specific team] hiring bar
- A pointer to whoever is closest to the [specific role] hiring decision

What does *not* work: “Can you refer me?” Asking for a referral as the first ask is a known pattern and recruiters route those messages to /dev/null. Referrals come after a real conversation, not before.

## What sounds like a tool-user

Recruiters and engineers can spot AI-shaped writing instantly now. Tells:

- “I hope this email finds you well.”
- “Leverage”, “synergy”, “innovative”, “passionate about”, “actionable insights”.
- Sentences that all have the same rhythm and length.
- Bullet points where there shouldn’t be bullet points.
- Cover-letter voice in a DM.

Write the way you talk. If you wouldn’t say “I am very interested in the opportunity to leverage my background” out loud to a friend, don’t put it in an email. “I want to work on infra and your team is the closest fit I’ve seen” is a sentence a person would say.

## Following up

One follow-up after 5 business days. Not “checking in” — restate the ask in fewer words. If they don’t reply to the follow-up, move on. They didn’t reply because they’re busy or not interested, both of which are fine, and a third nudge moves you from “polite student” to “spam”.

## A working template

This is the structure, not the words. Don’t copy it verbatim — adapt it.

> Subject: [Specific thing about their work]
>
> Hey [Name] — [one-line specific opener: connection, context, or curiosity].
>
> [One line about why you specifically: school, year, the thing you’re working on right now that’s relevant. No bio paragraph.]
>
> [The specific small ask, one sentence.]
>
> Thanks either way — I know you’re busy.
>
> [You]

Three paragraphs, maybe 80 words. If your draft is over 150 words, cut it in half and send the shorter version.

## The frame change

Cold outreach feels like begging when you write the begging template. It stops feeling like begging when you treat it as starting a real conversation with a peer about their work. The reply rate goes up. The conversations are better. Sometimes you get the internship; more often you get a useful relationship that pays off two cycles later.

Send fewer, better messages. Twenty good ones beats two hundred bad ones, and your blacklist stays clean.
