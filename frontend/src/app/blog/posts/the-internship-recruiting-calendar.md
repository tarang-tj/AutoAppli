It's mid-March. A junior at a state school is sitting in a library at 11pm scrolling LinkedIn, realizing for the first time that summer SWE intern applications at Google closed in October. Meta closed in September. Most of the companies they actually wanted are no longer accepting applications. The available openings are smaller companies, late-cycle startups, and the few large companies that didn't fill their req's in the first wave.

This is the most common pattern in undergrad CS recruiting and it's almost entirely a calendar mistake. Students treat internship search like job search — apply when you're ready — when the actual rule is the opposite. Internships are calendar-driven. The calendar starts almost a year before the internship.

Here's what the year actually looks like.

## August through October: Big Tech opens

The first wave of intern applications opens in late summer. If you're a sophomore or junior aiming at a Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, or Apple SWE intern role for next summer, the application window is roughly **late August through mid-October**. Some open earlier (Google has historically opened in mid-July for some programs); some hold req's open later. But the bulk of applications are reviewed in this window, and after October the openings either close or shift to a deprioritized batch.

Quant firms also open here. Citadel, Jane Street, HRT, Two Sigma, and Optiver run their internship recruiting in roughly the same August-October window. These firms move fast — some have full classes filled by October. If you're aiming quant, your resume should be tailored and submitted in the first week applications open, not the last.

**What you should be doing in July:** Resume already tailored to two or three target JDs. Referrals lined up. LinkedIn already updated. If it's August 1 and your resume is still the version you used for last summer, you're starting from behind.

## September through November: Most other large companies

The second wave opens about a month after Big Tech. Adobe, Salesforce, Cisco, Oracle, IBM, NVIDIA, banking SWE programs (JPM, Goldman, Morgan Stanley), and the next tier of consumer tech all run their windows here. Some run on a rolling basis; many run with hard deadlines in October or November.

This is also where most of the Fortune-500 SWE intern programs sit. They have larger intern classes than Big Tech (a JPM SWE intern class is in the hundreds) and the bar is correspondingly broader, but the window is real and applications submitted in December rarely surface.

**What you should be doing in August:** A list of 30-50 companies you actually want, sorted by application open date. Tracking deadlines is a separate skill from prepping applications, and most students forget the first one entirely.

## October through December: Mid-size tech and unicorns

Stripe, Airbnb, Coinbase, Snowflake, Databricks, Notion, Figma, Plaid, Ramp, Vercel — the public unicorns and pre-IPO companies — mostly open their intern req's in this window. They tend to run later than Big Tech for two reasons: smaller classes mean less urgency to fill, and many of them recruit from a smaller, more targeted pool where speed-to-fill is less critical.

This is also where companies that didn't hit their numbers in the first wave reopen reqs. A Big Tech company that closed applications in October but didn't fill their NYC office class might reopen a smaller batch of NYC-specific reqs in November. These second-wave reopenings are the best application windows of the year because the recruiter pool is smaller and you're competing against fewer applicants.

**What you should be doing in September:** Applications already in for any company on your A-list that opened in August. You should be running 5-10 applications per week, tailored, not 50 a week generic.

## November through February: Late wave for startups and smaller companies

Most startups under 500 people don't run a structured internship cycle. They open intern reqs when they need an intern, which is usually 2-3 months before they want them to start. For a summer intern, that's roughly November-February.

This is also where late-cycle Big Tech sometimes opens additional req's — a team that got headcount approval in Q4 might post an intern role in December that wasn't available in August. These are real openings; they're just hidden from the students who stopped applying after the first wave.

**Critical**: this is also when the "I missed the first wave" panic starts. Students who didn't apply in August suddenly realize the window is closing and start mass-firing applications in December. The smaller companies still hiring at this point have small recruiter teams and can spot panic-applying instantly. Your December applications should look more deliberate, not less, than your August ones.

**What you should be doing in October:** A second-wave list of 20-30 companies that historically open intern reqs in November-February. This list is harder to assemble (no big aggregator covers it well) but the application competition is dramatically lower.

## January through March: New-grad apps and on-campus recruiting

Two things happen in this window.

**First**, new-grad apps open at most large companies — January through March is the bulk of the cycle for full-time roles starting in summer or fall. This is a different cycle from intern recruiting and it has its own rules. If you're a senior, your January is for new-grad apps and your last-shot intern apps simultaneously.

**Second**, university on-campus recruiting (OCR) runs heavily through this window. Career fairs, info sessions, and OCR-specific application portals are how a non-trivial fraction of internships get filled at target schools. If your school has OCR, the calendar is set by your career center, not by the company — and the deadlines are usually 2-4 weeks ahead of the equivalent public application.

**What you should be doing in December:** A complete list of which target companies have closed their internship recruiting and which are still open. This list is the difference between productive January applications and panic January applications.

## March through May: Last chance for summer

By March, the summer internship market is mostly closed. The remaining openings:

- Small companies and startups that opened reqs late
- Replacement reqs at larger companies (someone backed out, a team got new headcount)
- Industries with later cycles: consulting (rolling), banking new-grad (mostly closed by now but some replacement req's), nonprofit and government (often April-June)
- International companies running on different calendars (some EU and Asian companies recruit in spring for summer)

If you're applying in March without anything lined up, your strategy shifts. Volume goes down (fewer reqs exist), tailoring quality goes up (every application matters more), and outreach becomes more important than applications because referrals at small companies are how most late-cycle slots get filled.

**What you should be doing in February:** If you're worried about being unplaced for summer, your February is for outreach to smaller companies, not for blasting more applications at companies whose reqs already closed.

## The mistake students make

Treating internship search like job search.

Job search is opportunistic. You see a posting, it fits, you apply. There's no calendar — companies hire when they need to hire. Most postings stay open for weeks or months, and "I'll apply next month" is usually fine.

Internship search is calendar-driven. The companies you want to work at have already decided how many interns they want for summer 2027 by August 2026. The req opens, applications come in for 6-10 weeks, and the recruiter ships a shortlist. After that, the window closes whether or not the req shows "still accepting applications" on LinkedIn.

The fix isn't to apply earlier — it's to plan earlier. By July of the year before your internship, you should know:

- Which 30-50 companies you want
- Which ones open in August vs September vs October
- Which ones have specific application deadlines vs rolling reviews
- Which referrals you have lined up at which companies
- What your resume looks like for each of the 3-5 role categories you're targeting

This is a lot of work. It's also the work that separates students who land their first-choice internships from students who scramble in March for whatever's still open.
