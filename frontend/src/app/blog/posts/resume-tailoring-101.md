It’s 1am. You’ve got 40 tabs open. The summer internship deadline at the company you actually want is in 18 hours and you haven’t tailored the resume yet. So you do what most students do — you Cmd-F the JD, find the words that aren’t already on your resume, and you sprinkle them on top. “Distributed systems.” “Agile.” “Cross-functional collaboration.” Save. Upload. Next.

That resume is going to lose to a worse candidate who tailored properly.

Here’s the part nobody tells you: ATS keyword matching matters way less than you think it does, and recruiters reading your bullets matter way more. A senior recruiter at a flagship tech company looks at maybe 200 resumes for an SWE intern req. They spend 30 seconds on yours. Half of that is your top role. The keywords get you past the first filter, sure — but the *bullets* are what get you the callback. And generic bullets get filed in the same pile as everyone else’s generic bullets.

Three patterns actually move callback rate. None of them are clever.

## 1. Mirror the JD’s tools — but only the ones you’ve actually used

Read the JD twice. Pull out the specific technical nouns: “Python”, “Postgres”, “Kubernetes”, “React”, “AWS Lambda”. Now look at your resume and ask: which of these have I touched? Not “I read about it” or “took a class that mentioned it” — actually shipped something with it.

Move those to the front. If the JD mentions Postgres in three places and your resume buries “PostgreSQL” in a parenthetical at the end of a bullet, fix that. Lead with it. Make the keyword match the JD’s exact word: if they say “Postgres”, don’t write “PostgreSQL” — write “Postgres”. Recruiters skim for exact-string matches and ATS filters can be that dumb too.

What you do *not* do is put “Kubernetes” on your resume because the JD asked for it and you watched a YouTube video about it once. The interviewer will ask. The follow-up question will eat you alive. Recruiters mark “exaggerated skills” mentally and it’s worse than not having the skill at all.

## 2. Reorder bullets so your top two in each role match the JD’s priorities

This is the move most students never make and the one that helps the most.

Take your most recent role on the resume. You probably have four or five bullets. The JD has a “responsibilities” or “you will” section — that’s the recruiter’s mental ranking of what they care about. Find the two of your bullets that map closest to the top two items in that ranking and *put them first*.

Recruiters read top-to-bottom and timebox themselves. The first two bullets get full attention. The bottom two might not get read at all. If your bullet about building a payments microservice is buried at the bottom and the JD is screaming “backend distributed systems experience preferred”, you’re losing to a candidate who put the same bullet first.

Same applies inside the project section. Three projects on your resume? Reorder them so the one closest to the JD’s tech stack is on top. Don’t lie. Just reshuffle.

## 3. Quantify the outcomes that the JD signals it cares about

“Improved performance” is meaningless. “Cut p95 latency from 800ms to 120ms across 12k req/min” is a sentence a recruiter will pause on.

But here’s the nuance: the *type* of number matters per JD. If the JD is for a perf-focused role, lead with latency, throughput, memory. If it’s data engineering, lead with rows processed, pipeline runtime, data volume. If it’s product engineering, lead with users affected, conversion lift, tickets closed. Same project, different number depending on the JD.

If you don’t have a number, you can usually infer one. “Migrated 8 services from REST to gRPC” is fine even if you don’t know the latency win. “Built the React frontend for an internal tool used by 200 engineers” is fine. Scope and scale are numbers too.

What you do *not* do: invent numbers. Recruiters and engineers can smell fabricated metrics from across the room. “Increased efficiency by 47%” reads as fake the second it lands.

## What not to do

Don’t keyword-stuff a “Skills” section with 40 technologies you barely know. Recruiters have seen this trick a thousand times and it actively hurts you.

Don’t paste the JD into ChatGPT and ask it to “tailor my resume”. The output reads like ChatGPT. Recruiters know what ChatGPT-shaped writing looks like by now and they’re not impressed.

Don’t include skills you can’t pass a 5-minute screen on. The interview is the resume’s promise; if the resume promised Kubernetes, the interview can ask about Kubernetes.

Don’t tailor by adding noise. Tailor by *reordering* and *trimming*. The best tailored resume is shorter than the generic one, not longer.

## The math on this

If you spend 10 minutes per application tailoring properly, and your callback rate goes from 4% to 12% (this is roughly what we see for internship-cycle students), you make 50 quality applications and get 6 callbacks. The student who mass-fires 200 untailored applications at a 1.5% callback rate gets 3 callbacks — and they spent more time, took more reputational hits with recruiters, and got fewer interviews.

Tailoring is 10 minutes. Mass-firing is faster but worse. That’s the whole game.
